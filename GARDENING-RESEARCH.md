## 1. Cosmos (Schmuckkörbchen)

**Botanical name:** *Cosmos bipinnatus*
**German name:** Schmuckkörbchen
**Type:** Annual
**Height:** 60–120 cm
**Bloom time:** July–October (first frost)

### Growing from Seed

| Detail | Info |
|--------|------|
| Sowing depth | 3–6 mm (1/4 inch) — barely cover with soil |
| Germination time | 7–10 days |
| Germination temp | 15–20°C (60–68°F) |
| Indoor start | 4–6 weeks before last frost (mid-April for Stuttgart) |
| Transplant outside | After May 15 (last frost date) |
| Spacing | 30–45 cm apart |
| Sun | Full sun (6+ hours) — perfect for Emma's balcony |

### Step-by-Step for Emma

1. **Fill seed tray** with Compo Bio soil, moisten the soil first
2. **Place seeds** on surface, press gently, cover with 3–6 mm of soil
3. **Mist gently** — don't soak, just keep evenly moist
4. **Place on sunny windowsill** — needs light and warmth to germinate
5. **Thin seedlings** when they have 2 sets of true leaves — keep the strongest
6. **Harden off** starting May 1 — set outside for increasing hours each day
7. **Transplant** into permanent pots after May 15

### Pro Tips

- **Pinch the tops** when seedlings are 30 cm tall — this makes them bushier with more blooms
- **Don't over-fertilize** — cosmos actually bloom MORE in lean soil. Too much nitrogen = lots of leaves, fewer flowers
- **Deadhead regularly** — snip spent flowers to keep new ones coming
- **They self-sow** — let a few flowers go to seed at the end of season for free plants next year
- **Stake tall varieties** — cosmos can get leggy; a simple bamboo stake helps in windy balcony conditions

### Container Growing

- **Minimum pot size:** 25–30 cm diameter, 25 cm deep
- **Drainage:** Essential — make sure pots have drainage holes
- **Soil mix:** Compo Bio + 30% coco coir for drainage
- **Watering:** Daily in hot summer weather; morning watering is best

### Common Problems

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Leggy seedlings | Not enough light | Move closer to window or outside |
| No flowers | Too much fertilizer/nitrogen | Cut back on feeding |
| Aphids | Common pest | Spray off with water; ladybugs eat them |
| Flopping over | Top-heavy or wind | Stake with bamboo stick |

### Research Links

- Royal Horticultural Society — Cosmos Growing Guide: https://www.rhs.org.uk/plants/cosmos/growing-guide
- Gardeners' World — How to Grow Cosmos: https://www.gardenersworld.com/how-to/grow-plants/how-to-grow-cosmos/
- Gartendialog (German) — Schmuckkörbchen pflegen: https://www.gartendialog.de/schmuckkoerbchen/

## 2. Zinnias (Field Mix)

**Botanical name:** *Zinnia elegans*
**Type:** Annual
**Height:** 30–90 cm (depends on variety in mix)
**Bloom time:** July–October

### Growing from Seed

| Detail | Info |
|--------|------|
| Sowing depth | 6 mm (1/4 inch) |
| Germination time | 5–7 days (fast!) |
| Germination temp | 20–25°C (68–77°F) — they like it warm |
| Indoor start | 4 weeks before last frost |
| Transplant outside | After May 15 |
| Spacing | 15–30 cm apart |
| Sun | Full sun (6+ hours) |

### Step-by-Step for Emma

1. **Sow in seed trays** — zinnias don't love being transplanted, so handle gently
2. **Keep warm** — they germinate best at room temperature or warmer
3. **Water from below** if possible — zinnias are prone to powdery mildew on wet leaves
4. **Transplant carefully** — try not to disturb roots
5. **Pinch the first flower bud** — this encourages the plant to branch and produce many more flowers

### Pro Tips

- **Water at soil level, not overhead** — this prevents powdery mildew, the #1 zinnia problem
- **Space them for airflow** — don't crowd pots, air circulation prevents disease
- **Deadhead for continuous blooms** — cut stems long for bouquets! Cutting IS deadheading
- **They love heat** — the hotter the summer, the happier zinnias are
- **Field mix = surprise colors** — you'll get a random assortment of colors and sizes, which is half the fun

### Container Growing

- **Minimum pot size:** 20–25 cm diameter per plant
- **Drainage:** Critical — zinnias hate soggy roots
- **Soil mix:** Compo Bio + coco coir, same as cosmos
- **Fertilize:** Every 2 weeks with balanced liquid fertilizer once blooming starts

### Common Problems

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Powdery mildew (white coating on leaves) | Overhead watering, poor airflow | Water at soil level, improve spacing |
| No flowers | Young plant or too much shade | Wait + ensure full sun |
| Stunted growth | Cold temperatures | Zinnias stall below 15°C — wait for warm weather |
| Caterpillars | Common garden pest | Pick off by hand |

### Research Links

- Royal Horticultural Society — Zinnia: https://www.rhs.org.uk/plants/zinnia
- The Spruce — How to Grow Zinnias: https://www.thespruce.com/how-to-grow-zinnias-1402827
- Mein schöner Garten (German) — Zinnien: https://www.mein-schoener-garten.de/pflanzen/zinnie

## 3. Lavender (Duft-Lavendel, Munstead)

**Botanical name:** *Lavandula angustifolia* 'Munstead'
**German name:** Duft-Lavendel
**Type:** Perennial (comes back every year!)
**Height:** 30–45 cm
**Bloom time:** June–August
**Hardiness:** Zone 5–8 (survives Stuttgart winters with protection)

### What Emma Has

- **Lavandula angustifolia** starter plant — planted April 26 in reserved grey pot on the Zone B sunny long wall
- **Soil mix:** 60% OBI Kakteenerde / 40% Compo Sana — grittier than the Sempervivum blend, more drainage for Mediterranean roots
- **Crown placement:** at/above soil line — buried crown = rot (#1 lavender killer)
- **Top-dress:** 5–10mm gravel (same as Sempervivum pots)
- **Fertilizer:** none — lean soil preference
- **Note:** L. stoechas (Schopf-Lavendel / French lavender) was skipped — only hardy to -5 to -10°C, won't survive Stuttgart winters. L. angustifolia (Echter Lavendel) is hardy to -20°C.

### Growing from Seed — The Patient Gardener's Guide

| Detail | Info |
|--------|------|
| Sowing depth | Surface sow — seeds need light to germinate! |
| Germination time | 14–28 days (sometimes longer!) |
| Germination temp | 18–21°C (65–70°F) |
| Indoor start | 10–12 weeks before last frost (February–March ideal, but April still works) |
| Transplant outside | After May 15 |
| Sun | Full sun (6+ hours) |

### Why Lavender from Seed Is Tricky (and Worth It)

Lavender is the slowest of Emma's seeds — and that's completely normal. Here's what to expect:

- **Week 1–2:** Nothing visible. Seeds are softening and preparing to sprout.
- **Week 2–4:** Tiny, tiny sprouts emerge. They look like grass at first.
- **Month 2–3:** Small rosettes of silvery-green leaves form. Still very small.
- **Month 4–6:** Noticeable growth. May or may not bloom in Year 1.
- **Year 2:** Strong plant with reliable blooms!

> **Don't give up!** Lavender from seed is a long game, but Munstead is one of the most reliable varieties for home growers.

### Step-by-Step for Emma

1. **Moisten Compo Bio soil** and fill seed tray
2. **Sprinkle seeds on surface** — do NOT cover with soil (they need light!)
3. **Press seeds gently** into soil with your finger
4. **Mist with spray bottle** — keep moist but never soggy
5. **Cover tray with plastic wrap** to retain moisture — remove once seeds sprout
6. **Place in bright, warm spot** — sunny windowsill is perfect
7. **Be patient** — check daily, mist when dry, and wait
8. **Transplant** when seedlings have several sets of leaves and are sturdy enough to handle

### Pro Tips

- **DO NOT overwater** — lavender is Mediterranean and likes it on the dry side
- **Good drainage is essential** — extra coco coir in the soil mix helps
- **Munstead is compact** — perfect for balcony pots, won't get too big
- **Prune after flowering** — cut back by 1/3 to keep it bushy and healthy
- **Can overwinter on the balcony** — wrap pot in burlap or bubble wrap for insulation, push against the building wall

### Harvesting & Uses

- **Cut stems** when flowers are just starting to open — most fragrant at this stage
- **Dry in bundles** hung upside down in a warm, dry spot
- **Uses:** Sachets for drawers, potpourri, herbal tea, cooking (lavender shortbread!), bath bags
- **Fragrance:** Munstead is specifically bred for strong scent — "Duft" means fragrance in German!

### Container Growing

- **Pot size:** 20–30 cm diameter (one plant per pot)
- **Drainage:** THE most important factor — add gravel to pot bottom, mix extra coir into soil
- **Soil mix:** Compo Bio + 40% coco coir + a handful of perlite if available
- **Watering:** Let soil dry between waterings. Stick your finger 2 cm in — if dry, water. If moist, wait.
- **Fertilize:** Very lightly, once in spring — lavender prefers lean soil

### Research Links

- Royal Horticultural Society — Lavandula angustifolia 'Munstead': https://www.rhs.org.uk/plants/10199/lavandula-angustifolia-munstead/details
- Gardenia — Lavandula Munstead: https://www.gardenia.net/plant/lavandula-angustifolia-munstead
- Gartenjournal (German) — Lavendel aus Samen ziehen: https://www.gartenjournal.net/lavendel-aus-samen-ziehen

## 4. Brodiaea Queen Fabiola

**Botanical name:** *Triteleia laxa* 'Queen Fabiola' (formerly *Brodiaea*)
**Type:** Bulb (corm)
**Height:** 40–50 cm
**Bloom time:** June–August
**Color:** Violet-blue star-shaped flowers in clusters

### What Emma Has

- 25-pack of bulbs, now planted in 2 gray railing planters
- Approximately 8–10 bulbs per planter, planted 8–10 cm deep

### Care After Planting

| Detail | Info |
|--------|------|
| Watering | When top 2 cm of soil is dry — don't overwater |
| Sun | Full sun to partial shade |
| Sprouting | Green shoots appear 4–6 weeks after planting |
| Blooming | June–August in clusters |
| After blooming | Let foliage die back naturally (feeds the bulb for next year) |
| Winter | Leave in pots; they're hardy to Zone 6 |

### Pro Tips

- **Don't cut the leaves** after flowering — let them yellow and die back on their own. This feeds the bulb for next year's blooms
- **Reduce watering** after blooms fade and leaves yellow
- **They multiply!** Each bulb can produce offsets over time — more flowers each year
- **Pair with** low-growing flowers like pansies around the base for a layered look

## 6. Basil (Basilikum)

**Botanical name:** *Ocimum basilicum*
**German name:** Basilikum
**Type:** Annual herb
**Height:** 30–60 cm
**Harvest:** Continuous from spring through fall

### Growing on the Balcony

| Detail | Info |
|--------|------|
| Sun | Full sun (6+ hours) — the more sun, the more flavor |
| Soil | Rich, well-draining potting mix (brown Compo is perfect) |
| Watering | Keep consistently moist — doesn't like drying out |
| Feeding | Light liquid fertilizer every 2–3 weeks |
| Temperature | Loves heat — struggles below 10°C |
| Frost hardy | No! Bring inside if nights dip below 5°C |

### How to Keep It Bushy

This is the single most important basil tip: **pinch from the top, always.**

1. Once basil has 3 sets of leaves, pinch off the top set
2. This forces two new branches to grow from below
3. Repeat every time a stem gets 3 new sets of leaves
4. **Never let it flower** — flowering makes leaves bitter and signals the plant to stop growing
5. If you see flower buds forming, pinch them off immediately

### Harvesting

- **Always pick from the top** — this encourages branching
- **Never strip more than 1/3** of the plant at once
- **Morning harvest** has the strongest flavor (oils are most concentrated before the heat of the day)
- **Use fresh** or make pesto and freeze — dried basil is a sad shadow of the fresh version

### Common Problems

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Wilting in the afternoon | Hot sun, needs water | Water in the morning, check again midday |
| Yellow lower leaves | Overwatering or natural aging | Let soil dry slightly, remove yellow leaves |
| Flowers appearing | Plant is maturing | Pinch off immediately! |
| Leggy/tall growth | Not enough pinching | Pinch tops regularly |

### Research Links

- Gardeners' World — How to Grow Basil: https://www.gardenersworld.com/how-to/grow-plants/how-to-grow-basil/
- Mein schoner Garten — Basilikum: https://www.mein-schoener-garten.de/pflanzen/basilikum

## 7. Chocolate Mint

**Botanical name:** *Mentha x piperita* 'Chocolate'
**Type:** Perennial herb
**Height:** 30–60 cm
**Harvest:** Continuous spring through fall
**Special feature:** Dark stems and leaves with a chocolate-mint scent!

### The Golden Rule: Mint Lives Alone

**NEVER plant mint with other plants.** Mint spreads via underground runners (stolons) and will completely take over any shared container within a month. Emma's chocolate mint is correctly planted in its own 27cm TEDI pot. This is not optional — it's survival for everything else on the balcony.

### Growing on the Balcony

| Detail | Info |
|--------|------|
| Sun | Full sun to partial shade — versatile |
| Soil | Any decent potting mix — mint is not picky |
| Watering | Keep consistently moist — mint likes more water than most plants |
| Feeding | Light fertilizer once a month at most — mint grows aggressively on its own |
| Frost hardy | Yes! Mint is a tough perennial that comes back every year |
| Winter | Can stay outdoors — wrap pot in burlap for extra protection |

### Harvesting & Uses

- **Tea:** Fresh mint leaves + hot water = instant herbal tea
- **Mojitos:** Muddle leaves, add rum, lime, soda, ice. Summer on the balcony!
- **Desserts:** Garnish for chocolate cake, ice cream, brownies — chocolate mint is made for this
- **Cooking:** Add to salads, spring rolls, lamb dishes
- **Harvest often** — the more you cut, the bushier it grows
- **Cut stems back to 1/3** in early summer to keep it compact and fresh

### Pro Tips

- **Trim regularly** to prevent it from getting leggy and woody
- **It's nearly unkillable** — if it looks dead, water it and wait. It almost always comes back
- **Propagation:** Stick a stem cutting in water — roots will appear in days. Infinite mint!
- **The chocolate scent** is strongest on warm sunny days and when you brush the leaves

### Research Links

- RHS — Growing Mint: https://www.rhs.org.uk/herbs/mint/grow-your-own
- Gardeners' World — How to Grow Mint: https://www.gardenersworld.com/how-to/grow-plants/how-to-grow-mint/

- **Pair with lavender** aesthetically — similar low-water, sun-loving personality

Emma has 2 pink dianthus waiting for a smaller hanging planter (shopping trip April 18). Plant with:

## 10. Sempervivum (Houseleek)

**Botanical name:** *Sempervivum* spp.
**Common names:** Houseleek, Hens and Chicks, Steinrose (German)
**Type:** Evergreen succulent perennial
**Height:** 5–15 cm (rosettes), flower stems to 30 cm
**Hardiness:** Zone 3–8 — extremely cold-hardy, perfect for Stuttgart winters

### What Emma Has

- Multiple Sempervivum rosettes, to be planted 3 per anchor pot around the rim
- Will share anchor pots with cosmos/zinnia in the center (after May 15)

### Growing on the Balcony

| Detail | Info |
|--------|------|
| Sun | Full sun — the more the better. Sun brings out the reds, purples, and bronzes |
| Soil | **40% Compo Cactea / 60% Compo Sana** — lean, well-draining |
| Watering | Very sparingly — let soil dry fully between waterings. Drought-tolerant |
| Fertilizer | **NEVER.** This is the single most important rule |
| Drainage | Essential — diameter and drainage matter more than depth |
| Pot depth | 20cm+ is fine — they're rock-crevice plants that don't need depth |
| Top-dress | Gravel or small rocks (5–10mm) around rosettes |

### Why NEVER Fertilize

This is critical. Feeding Sempervivum causes:
- **Soft, weak growth** — loses the tight compact rosette form
- **Washed-out colors** — reds, purples, and bronzes fade to bland green
- **Leggy, open rosettes** — they stretch instead of staying compact
- **Rot risk** — soft tissue is more vulnerable to moisture damage
- **Reduced winter hardiness** — lush growth doesn't survive frost as well

The Compo Cactea soil's 8-week built-in nutrients are already more than they need. After that, they thrive on neglect.

### Pro Tips

- **More sun = better color.** The intense reds and purples come from UV stress — it's a good thing for Sempervivum
- **They multiply!** "Hens and chicks" — the main rosette (hen) sends out offsets (chicks) on runners
- **Nearly indestructible.** Heat, cold, drought, poor soil — they handle it all
- **Monocarpic flowering:** When a rosette flowers, that specific rosette dies afterward — but the chicks carry on. Don't panic!
- **Top-dress with gravel** to keep the crown dry and prevent rot at soil level

### Research Links

- Royal Horticultural Society — Sempervivum: https://www.rhs.org.uk/plants/sempervivum
- Mountain Crest Gardens — Sempervivum Care Guide: https://mountaincrestgardens.com/sempervivum-care/

## 11. Euonymus fortunei (Wintercreeper)

**Botanical name:** *Euonymus fortunei*
**Common names:** Wintercreeper, Spindelstrauch (German)
**Type:** Evergreen shrub
**Height:** 30–60 cm (container), can climb/trail
**Spread:** 30–60 cm
**Hardiness:** Zone 5–9 — fully hardy in Stuttgart

### What Emma Has

- 2x Euonymus fortunei — 1 golden-variegated, 1 white-variegated (matched pair for visual balance)
- Will be potted in repurposed black 27x24cm pots (former basil/mint pots)

### Growing on the Balcony

| Detail | Info |
|--------|------|
| Sun | Partial shade to full sun — variegated types need acclimation to full sun |
| Soil | 100% Compo Sana — likes richer, more moisture-retentive soil |
| Watering | Keep evenly moist — more water than succulents, less than herbs |
| Fertilizer | Light feed in growing season — half strength every 3-4 weeks |
| Pruning | Trim ratty leaves after settling, shape as needed |
| Winter | Fully hardy — stays on the balcony year-round |

### Why Separate Pots (Not in Anchor Pots)

Originally considered joining Euonymus to the Sempervivum + cosmos/zinnia anchor pots. Rejected because:
- Euonymus is a woody shrub with 30-60cm spread — would crowd out everything
- Wants richer, more moisture-retentive soil (opposite of what Sempervivum needs)
- Separate pots allow independent watering and placement

### Variegated Leaf Care

- **Scorch risk:** Variegated leaves (especially white-variegated) can scorch in sudden full sun
- **Acclimate 1-2 days** in partial shade before moving to full sun position
- **Reversion:** If plain green shoots appear, prune them out — they'll outcompete the variegated growth
- **Less chlorophyll** in variegated leaves means slightly slower growth — that's normal

### Pro Tips

- **Year-round structure** — Euonymus provides evergreen interest when annuals are dormant
- **Can trail or climb** — versatile habit, can cascade over pot edges
- **Low-maintenance** — once established, they're very easy-going
- **Prune in spring** to keep compact and bushy
- **Matched pair** (golden + white variegated) creates visual balance on the balcony

### Research Links

- Royal Horticultural Society — Euonymus fortunei: https://www.rhs.org.uk/plants/euonymus-fortunei

## 12. Soil & Growing Media

### What Emma Has

#### Compo Bio Anzucht- und Krautererde (Brown Bag)
- **Type:** Peat-free seed starting and herb soil
- **Best for:** Starting all seeds, repotting all flowers
- **Why it's good:** Fine texture, good moisture retention, organic, peat-free
- **pH:** Slightly acidic to neutral (~6.0–6.5) — perfect for most flowers
- **Use as:** Base soil for everything

#### Neudorff Kokohumus (Coco Coir Brick)
- **Type:** Compressed coconut fiber
- **How to use:** Soak brick in water — it expands to 7–8x its size
- **Mix ratio:** ~30% coir to 70% Compo Bio soil
- **Benefits:**
  - Improves drainage (prevents waterlogging)
  - Retains moisture evenly (less frequent watering)
  - Lightweight (good for balcony — less weight on railing planters)
  - Sustainable alternative to peat
- **For lavender:** Use 40% coir for extra drainage

#### Compo Sana Qualitats-Blumenerde
- **Type:** Rich general-purpose flower soil
- **Best for:** Euonymus, Dianthus, cosmos/zinnia cell transplants, general potting
- **Stock:** 15L on hand + 20L arriving April 25 (Michael from OBI)
- **Use as:** Standard potting soil for most plants; 60% component in anchor pot blend

#### Compo Cactea (Peat-Free, 5L Bags)
- **Type:** Peat-free cactus and succulent soil with 8 weeks built-in nutrients
- **Best for:** Sempervivum (in anchor pot blend)
- **Stock:** 3x 5L = 15L on hand
- **Use as:** 40% component in anchor pot blend (40% Cactea / 60% Compo Sana)
- **Note:** The built-in 8-week nutrients are already more than Sempervivum needs — no additional feeding!

#### Compo BIO Blumendünger 1.3L
- **Type:** Organic flower fertilizer with potassium ("Mit Kalium")
- **Best for:** Dianthus, cosmos, zinnias, Euonymus — all flowering plants except Sempervivum
- **Dosage:** Half strength, every 2-3 weeks
- **Why chosen:** Flower-specific (potassium = more blooms, less leafy growth), BIO/organic = gentler with less burn risk, €10.99 for 1.3L = good value
- **NEVER use on Sempervivum**

#### Orchid Soil (White/OBI Bag)
- **DO NOT USE FOR FLOWERS**
- **Why:** Chunky bark-based mix designed for orchid roots that need air
- **Save for:** A future indoor orchid project!

### Soil Mixing Guide

| Use Case | Recipe |
|----------|--------|
| Seed starting | 100% Compo Bio (fine texture needed for tiny seeds) |
| Repotting flowers | 70% Compo Bio + 30% coco coir |
| Lavender pots | 60% Compo Bio + 40% coco coir |
| Brodiaea planters | 70% Compo Bio + 30% coco coir |

## 13. Balcony Container Gardening Essentials

### Pot Selection Guide

| Material | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|----------|------|------|----------|
| **Plastic** | Lightweight, cheap, retains moisture | Can look cheap, less breathable | Railing planters, starter pots |
| **Terracotta** | Beautiful, breathable, heavy (stable) | Dries out faster, heavy, can crack in frost | Lavender, herbs, display pots |
| **Glazed ceramic** | Attractive, retains moisture | Heavy, expensive | Statement pots for the balcony |
| **Fabric grow bags** | Excellent drainage, air-pruning roots | Dry out fast, not the prettiest | Temporary growing, starting plants |

### Drainage Is Everything

- **ALWAYS use pots with drainage holes** — no exceptions
- **Add a layer of pebbles or broken pottery** to the bottom of large pots
- **Use pot saucers** to catch runoff (but empty them — don't let pots sit in water)
- **Coco coir in the mix** helps with drainage

### Balcony Weight Considerations

- Railing planters should not be too heavy — plastic is best
- Use lightweight soil mixes (coco coir helps)
- Distribute heavy pots on the floor of the balcony, not the railing
- Check balcony weight limits if going big with many pots

### Fertilizing Schedule

| When | What | How |
|------|------|-----|
| Seed starting (April) | None needed — starter soil has enough nutrients | — |
| After transplanting (late May) | Balanced liquid fertilizer (e.g., 10-10-10 or organic flower feed) | Every 2 weeks |
| Peak bloom (June–August) | Continue liquid fertilizer | Every 2 weeks |
| Late season (September) | Stop fertilizing | Plants winding down |
| Lavender | Very light fertilizer, once in spring | Less is more! |
