## Fun Project Ideas

### 1. Balcony Herb Corner
Add a few herb pots alongside the flowers! Great options for a sunny Stuttgart balcony:
- **Basil** — easy, smells amazing, useful in cooking
- **Rosemary** — pairs beautifully with lavender visually
- **Thyme** — low-growing, pretty purple flowers
- **Mint** — keep in its own pot (it takes over otherwise!)
- **Chives** — pretty purple flower heads, great in salads

### 2. DIY Seed Saving Station
In late summer (August–September), collect seeds from the cosmos and zinnias to replant next year. All you need is:
- Paper envelopes or small bags
- A marker to label varieties
- A dry, cool spot to store them over winter

Free flowers forever!

### 3. Butterfly & Bee Garden
The current plant selection already attracts pollinators! To level up:
- Add a shallow dish of water with pebbles (bee watering station)
- Plant more lavender — bees love it
- Add a butterfly bush in a large pot (Buddleja — blooms all summer)
- Marigolds and zinnias are already butterfly magnets

### 4. Balcony Trellis / Vertical Garden
Maximize space by growing upward:
- Install a small trellis or wire frame against the wall
- Grow climbing flowers: sweet peas, morning glories, or black-eyed Susan vine
- Hang small pots on the trellis for even more flowers

### 5. Evening / Night Garden
Add plants that glow in the evening or smell amazing at night:
- **Nicotiana (Flowering Tobacco)** — white varieties glow at dusk, sweet fragrance at night
- **Moonflower** — dramatic white blooms that open at sunset
- **Night-scented Stock** — plain by day, incredibly fragrant at night
- Add fairy lights or solar-powered string lights for ambiance

### 6. Pressed Flower Art
Once blooms are plentiful:
- Press flowers in heavy books (cosmos press beautifully!)
- Make framed pressed flower art
- Create bookmarks or greeting cards
- Great gift idea!

### 7. Indoor Winter Garden
When the balcony season ends (October/November):
- Bring lavender indoors (it can survive inside near a sunny window)
- Start an indoor herb garden
- Use that orchid soil for an actual orchid!
- Force spring bulbs indoors in December for winter blooms (hyacinths are perfect)

### 8. Garden Journal / Photo Book
- Take a photo of the balcony from the same angle every week
- Create a timelapse or photo collage showing the transformation
- Keep notes on what worked and what didn't for next year
- This GitHub repo is already a great start!

## Patio Corner — Potted Tree Options

**Location:** Sunny patio corner, sheltered overhead by wood beam structure, stone tile floor, strong direct sun.
**Approach:** Potted only — no climbers, no ground planting (rental unit + annual contract renewals = season-by-season strategy).
**Status:** Scouting / future addition — **not urgent**, pull up next time the OBI itch strikes.

### Top Candidates

| Pick | Vibe | Care | Winter | Pros | Cons |
|------|------|------|--------|------|------|
| 🫒 **Olive** *(Michael's pick)* | Mediterranean, sculptural, silver-green | Low — drought-tolerant | Hardy in Stuttgart with fleece on coldest nights; stays in pot outside | Low maintenance, evergreen-ish, portable, ages beautifully | Slow growing, won't fruit reliably (decorative only) |
| 🍋 **Meyer Lemon** *(Emma's pick)* | Romantic, fragrant blooms, real fruit | High — citrus-specific feed, careful watering | **Must come inside below ~5°C** (Oct–Apr) — needs bright cool indoor spot | Fragrant flowers, edible fruit, dramatic statement | Significant work, indoor winter space, fussy |
| 🍊 **Calamondin Orange** | Smaller, decorative ornamental fruit | Same as Meyer lemon | Indoor winter required | Smaller footprint than lemon | Same indoor winter requirement |

### Lower-Maintenance Alternatives

- **Bay Laurel (Lorbeer)** — evergreen, fragrant, edible. Tolerates Stuttgart winters with minor protection. Sculptural.
- **Fig Tree** — surprisingly hardy in Stuttgart. Dramatic large leaves, cottage-garden feel. Edible fruit. Deciduous (bare in winter).
- **Dwarf Olive Standard / Topiary Ilex crenata** — architectural single-statement. Ilex crenata = boxwood substitute (already preferred over boxwood for moth reasons). Year-round structure.

### Decision Logic (When OBI-Ready)

- Want **low-effort + Mediterranean look?** → Olive
- Want **fragrance, fruit, and don't mind the work?** → Meyer Lemon
- Neither at OBI? → Bay laurel or fig as backup statement plants

### Constraints (Locked)

- ✅ Must be portable in a pot (rental + potential relocation)
- ✅ Must work with season-by-season approach (no multi-year commitments)
- ✅ Drainage non-negotiable
- ✅ Terracotta / stone-look pot aesthetic
- ❌ No boxwood, no Lavandula stoechas (existing rules)
- ⚠️ If choosing citrus: confirm indoor winter spot exists **before** purchase

> Reference compiled April 29, 2026. See [Gardening Research → Section 19](GARDENING-RESEARCH#19-patio-corner-potted-tree-candidates) for full care detail.

## Fun Facts for Emma

- Cosmos means "order" or "beauty" in Greek — the flowers were named for their perfectly ordered petals
- Lavender has been used for over 2,500 years — the Romans used it in their baths (the name comes from Latin "lavare" = to wash)
- Zinnias were the first flowers ever grown in space! NASA grew them on the International Space Station in 2016
- Stuttgart's climate (Zone 7b/8a) is actually excellent for most European garden flowers
